Classification analysis of Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Background

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) is a critical medical condition characterized by sudden and severe lung inflammation, leading to decreased oxygen levels in the blood. With its high mortality rate and challenges in diagnosis and treatment, ARDS presents a significant burden to healthcare systems worldwide. The COVID-19 pandemic has further highlighted the importance of understanding and managing ARDS cases.

A new treatment to improve the disease outcome for ARDS patients is the Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation (ECMO) therapy. Although ECMO is a highly complex therapy, its success in saving lives and improving patient outcomes has made it an indispensable tool in critical care settings around the world.

Research focus

Over the past decade, several clinical trials have been conducted to evaluate the benefits of ECMO compared to conventional management for severe respiratory failure. The results have been mixed, and the efficacy of ECMO can depend on factors such as patient selection criteria, the underlying condition, etc. ECMO is a highly specialized and resource-intensive treatment that carries significant risks and complications. Therefore, it should be reserved for patients who are most likely to benefit from the treatment.

In this project we analysed medical records of 450 ARDS patients who have undergone ECMO therapy. Each individual record encompassed a multitude of biomarkers alongside a clinical outcome. The aim of this project is to investigate the association between specific biomedical markers and the suitability of ARDS patients for ECMO treatment.The goal is predict whether a patient will survive the ECMO treatment and to identify relevant biomarkers for assessing treatment outcomes.

Dataset

The dataset used for this study contain biomarker data of 450 ARDS patients. There were 145 women and 305 men among the patients, and their ages ranged from 17 to 83. Of the 450 patients, 341 (75.8%) survived ECMO treatment and 109 (24.2%) did not survive the treatment. Furthermore, the dataset contain 33 variables: 2 binary, one categorical and 30 continuous.

Twenty continuous variables out of the 33 variables contain missing values. The variable with the largest number of missing values in our dataset is albumin (46%). Since no explanation was provided about their absence, we eliminated the variable 'albumin' from the analysis.

Beyond the definitions of some of the features, no further details about the dataset or the features included were provided in the dataset manuscript.

Methodology

Healthcare data often contains highly correlated variables. Such multicollinearity in data can cause problems with developing prediction models. Moreover, the lesser the number of predictors the more the possibility of obtaining a simpler and more understandable model. Due to that reason we initiated the analysis with feature selection algorithms like hypothesis testing, Lasso regression, and Boruta algorithm.

Machine Learning (ML) models are widely used in clinical data analysis due to its ability to uncover complex patterns and identify subtle relationships. Therefore, following feature selection, we employed ML algorithms for prediction. We explored five machine learning algorithms; logistic regression, Naive Bayes, decision trees, random forest, and neural networks. Each algorithm was evaluated based on performance metrics such as Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC), F1 score, and accuracy.

Data preprocessing played a crucial role in ensuring the integrity and robustness of our analyses. Techniques for handling missing values and addressing data imbalance (Random Under-Sampling) were meticulously applied to optimize model performance. Model evaluation was conducted on both imbalanced and balanced datasets.

Results

Correlations in the dataset were evaluated using both the Pearson correlation coefficient (parametric test) and the Spearman correlation coefficient (non parametric test). If the correlation between variables exceeded 0.7, we retained only one of the variables to avoid problems when implementing prediction models. In our case, the variable with the highest number of missing values would be removed. Due to the relatively less number of missing values in the dataset, we replaced it with the median of the corresponding column. The imputed dataset contain 29 features; 26 continuous, two binary and one categorical.

Lasso regression performs automatic variable selection by setting the coefficients of irrelevant variables to zero. Out of the 34 explanatory variables in our dataset, lasso selected five variables as important (see fig.1.1).

Besides lasso, we also used Boruta for feature selection because it can capture non-linear relationships and interactions in the data. Boruta identified eight variables as important in our dataset (see fig 1.2).

Following feature selection, we used ML algorithms for generating prediction results. The MCC, F1 score and accuracy of the ML models are given in table 1.1. In general, all the methods achieved good accuracy. The models applied to variables selected by feature selection algorithms performed slightly better than the full model.Random forest achieved high scores in all the three evaluation metrics while Naive Bayes (full model) attained lowest scores.

Even though we obtained generally good model accuracy on complete dataset, the MCC and F1 scores remained comparatively low. This means that these classifiers are no better than a random guess classifier. We believe that this is due to data imbalance and therefore we addressed this issue with the under-sampling technique.

Numerous techniques exist for managing data imbalance. The issue of data imbalance in our dataset was tackled using the Random Under-Sampling (RUS) technique. RUS was chosen due to its simplicity, speed, and the fact that it does not involve manipulation of data instances. To implement this technique, we created a balanced dataset such that it contain all the instances from the minority class and randomly selected instances from the majority class. After that, we applied all prediction methods to this under-sampled dataset and the results obtained are given in table 1.2.

Compared to the previous results, here all methods achieved better F1 scores but lower accuracy, possibly stemming from information loss. Similar to previous results, the models applied to variables selected by feature selection algorithms performed slightly better than the full model. But, decision trees applied to Boruta-selected variables exhibited inferior performance in comparison to the full model. Neural networks outperformed all the other models in terms of all the three metrics (MCC: 0.72, F1: 0.81, accuracy: 0.88).

Discussion

Our feature selection algorithms identified lactate, chemotherapy status, and respiratory rate as significant variables. The noteworthy role of lactate levels in ECMO treatment gains further validation from established research articles. This suggests that doctors should consider the values of these biomarkers when assessing the suitability of ECMO therapy for a patient.

For construction of binary classification models for ECMO survival, for similar analyses, it is advisable to consider using random forest for imbalanced datasets and neural networks for datasets with a more balanced distribution.

An important point to highlight is the substantial variability in these evaluation metrics across different runs. Notably, neural networks consistently maintained high MCC, F1 score, and accuracy on the under-sampled dataset, outperforming other models. It is advisable to consider the average of these metrics for a more comprehensive assessment of model effectiveness. In the future, we aim to utilize average metric values to enhance model evaluation and explore the reasons for this observed variability.

Although under-sampling addressed data imbalance, it came at the cost of losing valuable information. To enhance our approach, we also plan to explore different methods for handling data imbalance in future work, aiming to strike a better balance between preserving information and addressing the imbalance.
